Monty Hall banega Crorepati - Part 1

Application Of Probability Theory to a popular game show

Now in its 15th season, hosted by Amitabh Bacchan, the game show Kaun Banega Crorepati based on the british series who wants to be a millionaire; follows a quiz format where the contestant earns a progressively increasing financial reward for each correct answer. While the premise is simple, the reward structure of the game and ’lifelines’ or helper aids available to the contestant makes it an interesting candidate for further mathematical inspection.

Gameplay#

  • The host asks the participant a series of increasingly obscure trivia questions
  • For each correct answer the participant progresses to the next question, and receives a financial rewards based on the [[#Reward Structure]]
  • The participant is given 3 [[#Lifelines]] or aids which they may use during any stage of the game play
  • The participant may walk away from the game at any point while encashing the rewards they have received so far
  • Answering incorrectly ends the game for the participant and they walk away with the latest ‘Miss Answer Value’ for the question

Lifelines#

Fifty-Fifty / 50:50#

If a participant opts for the 50:50 lifeline, the host removes two of the 3 incorrect options leaving the participant to chose from the two remaining options. Effectively bringing the probability of guessing the correct answer up to $50%$.

While that is straight forward a mathematically enabled participant can use this lifeline to improve his odds of getting to the correct answer significantly. The key to the puzzle lies in the fact that if the participant opts for this lifeline the host has to remove two incorrect options.

Strategy#

The strategy is the participant should mentally chose one of the four options with $P(correct\ option) = \frac{1}{4}$ and $P(incorrect\ option) = \frac{3}{4}$). Then opt for this lifeline without making the host aware of this selection. The host then proceeds to remove two of the three incorrect option.

Case 1: Host Doesn’t remove the option chosen by the participant#

This may happen if the participant has chosen the correct option, with $probability\ 1$ since the host cannot reveal the correct option

OR

when the participant has chosen an incorrect option with $probability,\ \frac{2}{3} \times \frac{1}{2}$ ie. probability that participant’s option isn’t chosen in the first reveal multiplied by probability that the participant’s option isn’t chose in the second reveal

In this case the participant is left with the choice to switch to the other remaining option or stick to their original selection. This is akin to the [[Monty Hall Problem]] with 4 options instead of 3.

Here the participant chooses the correct option with probability $\frac{1}{4}$ and incorrect option with probability $\frac{3}{4}$.

Let $X$ = participant chose the wrong option $S$ = Participant switches their option after opting for 50:50

Hence, $P(winning| X,S) = P(X) \times P(Win|S)$

Substituting $P(X)=\frac{3}{4}$, and $P(Win|S) = P(selecting\ correct\ option) = 1$ since the only option remaining is the correct option.

$\implies\quad P(winning|X,S) = P(X) \times 1 = \frac{3}{4}$ $\therefore P(winning|case\ 1) = \frac{3}{4}$

Case 2: Host removes the option chosen by the participant#

This only occurs when the participant has chose the incorrect option, with probability $\frac{3}{4}$, the host can remove the participant’s chosen option in two ways.

  1. First option removed by the host is the option chosen by the participant, $p = \frac{1}{3}$
  2. Second option removed by the host is the option chosen by the participant, $p=\frac{2}{3}\times\frac{1}{2}$

$\implies$ Probability that the option chosen by the participant is removed by the host is $\frac{1}{3} + \frac{2}{3}\times\frac{1}{2} = \frac{2}{3}$

In this case, the participant has no option but to switch. Since two options are left of which one is the correct option, the $P(correct\ option)=\frac{1}{2}$

Hence, $P(winning|case 2) = \frac{1}{2}$

Conclusion#

By using this strategy the participant wins with a probability of

$P(winning) = P(winning|case\ 1)\times P(case\ 1) + P(winning|case\ 2)\times P(case\ 2)$

$\implies P(winning) = \frac{3}{4}\times \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{2}{3}= \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{3} = \frac{7}{12}$

Hence by employing this strategy the participant is $\frac{\frac{7}{12}}{\frac{1}{2}} = \frac{14}{12} = 1\frac{1}{6}$ times more likely to win the price.
